## Summary

Behavioral interviews accumulate folklore. Most of it is wrong, and the wrong advice fails predictably — vague pronouns, memorized scripts, throwaway "failures," and the assumption that warmth substitutes for evidence are the four most reliable ways to get down-leveled or no-hired. This doc names ten myths that experienced interviewers see every week, explains what the actual scoring rubric does with each, and points at the corrected mental model. Where a single corrected behavior would help most: stop saying "we," start saying "I," and quantify the result.

---

## 1. Myth: "Soft Skills Don't Matter for ICs"

**The myth:** Senior individual contributors are evaluated on technical depth. The behavioral round is a hurdle, not a signal — the recruiter just has to tick a box.

**Why it's wrong:** The behavioral round is where **levelling** is decided. Two engineers with identical coding-round and system-design scores routinely split on whether they get an L4 or L5 offer based on behavioral signal alone. At Amazon and Meta the bar raiser / cross-functional reviewer is *specifically* charged with checking that the candidate's behavioral evidence justifies the level; "great coder, no behavioral evidence at level" is a documented down-level reason at both employers.

**Empirical anchor:** Google's **Project Oxygen** (publicly published on `re:Work`) studied internal performance data and found that the top predictors of high-performing engineers and managers were behaviors — coaching, communication, vision, decision-making — not raw IQ or coding ability. The *whole* "Googleyness" rubric exists because Google's own data said behavior outperformed brilliance.

**Corrected model:** The behavioral round decides what level you join at and how fast you get promoted. Treat it accordingly.

## 2. Myth: "Just Memorize Five Answers"

**The myth:** Pick five strong stories, memorize them word-for-word, and rotate them across whatever question is asked.

**Why it's wrong:** Two failure modes appear within the first 90 seconds of a memorized answer.

1. **Mismatch between question and story.** Memorized answers force you to bend the answer to the question. A story about leading a migration, asked under a "tell me about a conflict" prompt, will not contain the conflict signal the rubric wants to see. Interviewers notice the mismatch ("tell me more about the conflict specifically") and the candidate's improvisation is then visibly worse than the memorized opening.
2. **Loss of follow-up depth.** Memorized answers do not survive contact with the follow-up tree. "Walk me through how you decided the rollback threshold" is a normal follow-up; if you cannot answer at the next layer of detail, the interviewer correctly infers the surface story was over-rehearsed.

A separate problem: rehearsed stories have rehearsed cadence. Trained interviewers — bar raisers especially — recognize the cadence and discount the score on the assumption the story is partially fabricated.

**Corrected model:** Build a **story bank** of 6–10 real events with rich actual detail (people, dates, dollar amounts, decision points, what went wrong). Memorize the *raw material*, not the speech. Compose the answer at the table. See [Story Banking](04-story-banking.md).

## 3. Myth: "Behavioral Is a Senior-Only Concern"

**The myth:** New grads and junior engineers don't need behavioral prep — there's nothing to ask about.

**Why it's wrong:** New-grad and L3 loops include behavioral questions, scaled to expected level. The level-3 rubric for "Ownership" might score "took initiative on assigned project," which a new grad can absolutely demonstrate from internships, school projects, or open source. The rubric is *not* "had multi-team impact" at L3; it is calibrated to the level you're applying for.

The myth produces a specific failure: junior candidates show up with no story bank because they assumed they didn't need one, then improvise on the spot, then deliver vague generalities, then get no-hired for "lack of behavioral evidence" — when they had the evidence, they just hadn't packaged it as STAR.

**Corrected model:** Whatever your level, draft 6–10 stories from the projects you actually have. Internship, school, open source, and side-project material is fully scoreable.

## 4. Myth: "Be Authentic Means Be Unprepared"

**The myth:** Preparing makes me sound rehearsed; better to wing it and show my real personality.

**Why it's wrong:** "Authentic" answers without prep tend to be vague, action-light, and missing quantification — exactly the three failure modes most likely to score low. Authenticity is not the absence of preparation; it is **using your real material at your normal speaking cadence**. Polished structure with real specifics is what high-scoring answers look like.

Compare:

> *Unprepared "authentic":* "I had this conflict once with my tech lead, and we kind of, you know, talked it through, and I think it ended up being fine. We learned a lot."

> *Prepared and authentic:* "Last June my tech lead wanted to ship the cache layer in front of Postgres before we'd run a load test. I'd just been on-call for the prior week's incident with the same pattern, so I drafted a one-page risk doc with three specific scenarios. We agreed on a 24-hour timeline to validate; the load test surfaced two bugs; we shipped three days later with no P0. The risk-doc template became team practice afterwards."

Both are "authentic." Only the second scores. The trained interviewer's job is to extract scoreable evidence; the prepared answer hands it to them.

## 5. Myth: "The Interviewer Wants the Right Answer"

**The myth:** Behavioral questions have a correct response and the interviewer will lead me toward it.

**Why it's wrong:** Behavioral questions ask for **your past behavior**, not the textbook answer. The interviewer is not testing whether you know what *should* have been done — they are scoring what you actually *did*. There is no answer key.

A common failure: the candidate, when asked about a conflict, describes what *they would do now in retrospect* rather than what they did at the time. The rubric scores past behavior. "Now I would…" answers produce zero ticks in the actions column.

**Corrected model:** Tell the story as it actually happened, including the parts that went wrong. The rubric rewards self-awareness and learning; the only way to score those is to be candid about what you actually did.

## 6. Myth: "If I'm Asked About a Failure I Should Pick a Small One"

**The myth:** A throwaway failure ("I once didn't comment my code well enough") protects me without revealing weakness.

**Why it's wrong:** "Tell me about a time you failed" is one of the most rubric-loaded questions in the loop. The rubric scores three things, in roughly this order:

1. **Was this an actual failure?** Throwaway answers signal you can't or won't acknowledge real failure — interpreted as low self-awareness or low ownership.
2. **Did you own it?** "We failed because the requirements were unclear" or "I failed because the deadline was unreasonable" both fail this check. The rubric wants the failure attributed to a decision you personally made.
3. **What changed because of it?** "I learned to be more careful" is too generic. The rubric wants a specific second-order change — a system, a checklist, a habit, a decision rule — that proves the lesson stuck.

Pick a real failure. Own it. Name what specifically changed in your practice. See [Handling Failure & Feedback](../question-bank/09-failure-and-feedback.md).

## 7. Myth: "I Should Use 'We' to Be a Team Player"

**The myth:** Saying "I" sounds arrogant; "we" is collaborative and modest.

**Why it's wrong:** "We" is the single most common failure mode in behavioral answers. The rubric wants to know **what *you personally* did**, not what the team did. "We decided to use a circuit breaker, we ran the tests, we shipped it" produces zero scoreable individual actions.

Trained interviewers will explicitly redirect: "You said *we*. What was *your* part of that?" That redirect is a documented signal that the answer was insufficiently specific. You can recover, but you've lost a minute and the interviewer has now flagged the answer as needing remedial probing.

**Corrected model:** Use "we" only for shared decisions and collaborative steps. Switch to "I" the moment you describe an action you personally took. A good answer alternates: *"The team agreed to investigate. **I** owned the load test… **I** wrote the risk doc… **I** brought the results to standup."* See [The STAR Framework](03-star-framework.md) section on action-voice.

## 8. Myth: "Charisma and Likability Decide It"

**The myth:** Get the interviewer to like you and the rest doesn't matter.

**Why it's wrong:** Charisma moves scores **within a level**, not **between levels**. Trained interviewers, bar raisers, and hiring committees are explicitly checked for "halo effect" bias and like-bias. Calibration sessions at large employers walk through transcripts and rate how often interviewers gave inflated scores to friendly-but-vague candidates; the data is used to retrain interviewers.

Friendly answers without evidence still score low. Slightly stiff answers with rich, specific evidence still score high. If you have to choose between rapport and rigor, choose rigor.

That said: **rapport without evidence is a tax on yourself, but evidence without rapport is also a tax**. The fully optimal candidate is warm *and* specific. Don't drop one to get the other; both are achievable with prep.

## 9. Myth: "Behavioral Is the Same Across Companies"

**The myth:** Generic STAR prep transfers across employers.

**Why it's wrong:** STAR transfers; the rubric does not. Amazon scores against the 16 Leadership Principles, and every behavioral question is mapped to one or two specific LPs. Google scores against GCA / Role-Related Knowledge / Leadership / Googleyness, with separate sub-rubrics. Meta scores against Execution / Drive / Direction / People (E5+ adds People). Microsoft uses Model-Coach-Care for managers and a related rubric for ICs.

Same story, told without rubric-aware framing, scores differently at each employer. The Amazon-aware version of the cache-vs-load-test story names "Bias for Action" and "Are Right, A Lot" in the actions; the Google version emphasizes "GCA" (general cognitive ability — your reasoning and decision process) and "Googleyness"; the Meta version foregrounds "Drive" (how you owned outcomes).

**Corrected model:** Per-employer prep is the highest-leverage activity in the final week before a loop. Map each story to the target rubric. See the Tier 3 docs (planned) for company-specific frameworks.

## 10. Myth: "If I Don't Have a Story I Should Make One Up"

**The myth:** A fabricated-but-plausible story scores better than admitting "I don't have a great example."

**Why it's wrong:** Fabricated stories fail the follow-up tree predictably. Trained interviewers ask "What was the load before? What was it after?" or "How did you measure success?" or "Who else did you talk to?" Fabricated stories run out of detail at the second or third follow-up. The interviewer's notes will record "could not provide specifics under follow-up" — which is read by hiring committees as a reliability flag, not a memory lapse.

A second risk: many large employers do reference checks during the offer stage that include verifying specific stories. Stories that contradict references kill offers at the finishing line.

**Corrected model:** If you genuinely don't have a strong example for a question, say so honestly and offer the closest adjacent story: *"I haven't led a multi-team initiative, but the closest I have is leading a cross-team migration on my own team. May I tell you about that instead?"* This scores well — it shows self-awareness and respects the interviewer's time. Trained interviewers will accept the substitute and probe it normally.
