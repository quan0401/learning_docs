## Summary

The four career-oriented questions almost always open and close a behavioral round. They are not throwaway icebreakers — they directly score *motivation*, *self-awareness*, and *fit*. The biggest mistakes are the same in all four: vagueness, complaint-shaped answers about your current employer, and generic "this company is great" praise that the interviewer has heard from every candidate that day. Strong answers in this category are short, specific, and reveal a candidate who has thought about their career like a system they're shaping deliberately.

## 1. "Tell Me About Yourself"

### 1.1 What this question is really asking

Three things, simultaneously: (a) can you communicate concisely under no time pressure, (b) what is the trajectory you're presenting, (c) what should I anchor my interpretation of the rest of the loop on. The interviewer often has 60 seconds of context on you (resume, recruiter brief) and uses your answer to update that picture. A bad answer here makes every later answer harder to interpret.

### 1.2 The competency signal

- **Communication.** Can you produce a tight 90-second narrative, or do you ramble?
- **Self-awareness.** Do you know what your career arc looks like from the outside?
- **Motivation.** Why this conversation, today?

### 1.3 The 90-second self-pitch — structure

| Beat | Length | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Where you are now | 15s | Current role, current employer, specific scope |
| One specific recent thing | 30s | A *specific* recent project or accomplishment with a number |
| Trajectory before now | 20s | Roles you held that led here, in 2 sentences max |
| Why this conversation | 20s | What about *this* role caught your interest |
| **Total** | **~85s** | |

### 1.4 STAR-flavored worked example

> "I'm currently a senior backend engineer at $employer, working on the payments-platform team — about 2.5k RPS at peak, ~12 services in Go and Java. The last big thing I shipped was a redesign of our idempotency layer; we cut duplicate-charge incidents from roughly 40 a month to under 5, with no regression in latency.
>
> Before $employer I was at $previous_employer for three years on the order-management side, and before that I started in a backend role at $smaller_employer right out of school. My career has trended toward systems where correctness under failure matters — payments, orders, anything where a retry can cost real money.
>
> I'm here today because $target_company's payments work has been on my radar for a while — specifically the [name a real, recent technical artifact: blog post, conference talk, OSS commit]. The combination of scale and correctness pressure is exactly the kind of problem I want to keep going deeper on."

Notes on what this does:

- One **specific number** (40 → 5). One **specific named system** (idempotency layer). One **specific reason for the conversation** that names a real artifact from the target company.
- 85 seconds, not 4 minutes.
- Trajectory in 2 sentences, not a full resume narration.
- No salary, no manager complaints, no "I'm passionate about technology."

### 1.5 Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why it scores low |
|---|---|
| Reading the resume aloud | The interviewer already has the resume |
| Going back to college | Burns the budget on irrelevant context |
| "I'm passionate about technology" | Empty filler; everyone is "passionate" |
| Naming personal hobbies first | Mismatched register for a technical loop |
| Going over 2 minutes | Signals weak self-editing |
| Generic "great company" closer | Doesn't anchor the rest of the round |

### 1.6 Follow-ups to expect

- "Tell me more about the idempotency redesign" — this is the natural pivot into a Project Based answer; have it ready (see [Project Based](02-project-based.md))
- "What made you go from $previous_employer to $employer?" — bridges into the "why leaving" question
- "What are you looking for next?" — bridges into the "next-role priorities" question

## 2. "Why Are You Leaving Your Current Role?"

### 2.1 What this question is really asking

Whether you'll leave the *next* role for the same reasons. The interviewer is calibrating retention risk. They are also checking your judgement: a candidate who badmouths their current employer signals they will badmouth this one in 18 months.

### 2.2 The competency signal

- **Judgment.** Are your reasons reasonable, specific, and forward-looking?
- **Earn Trust / Maturity.** Can you describe a leaving without being a complaint?
- **Self-awareness.** Are the things you're moving toward consistent with what this role actually offers?

### 2.3 The diplomatic departure narrative

Pattern: **acknowledge what was good → name a concrete gap → connect the gap to what this role offers**.

> "I've spent three years at $employer and learned a lot about $domain — the team, the product, and the on-call discipline have been strong. The gap I've started to feel is around scale: our peak load is about 3k RPS, and the kind of correctness-under-load problems I want to work on next start showing up at the 50k+ RPS range. That's the specific reason this team caught my attention."

Three things this does:

1. **Acknowledges** what was good about the current role (without grovelling).
2. **Names a concrete, forward-looking gap** — scale, in this case. Not "my manager was bad."
3. **Connects** the gap to *this* role specifically.

### 2.4 Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Manager complaints | Scores low on Earn Trust and reads as retention risk |
| Compensation as the reason | Honest but signals you'll leave again for more comp |
| "I want a new challenge" | Empty; what challenge specifically? |
| RIF / layoff handled awkwardly | Acknowledge it factually, redirect forward; see below |
| Bashing the company | Worst possible signal; interviewer assumes you'll do the same here |
| Vagueness ("I'm just looking around") | Reads as low motivation |

**For layoff or RIF situations:** be matter-of-fact. *"My team was part of the May restructuring at $employer. The work I'd been doing on $project ended; I started looking, and your team's $area work was the strongest match for what I want to do next."* Trained interviewers do not penalize a layoff. They do penalize an awkward attempt to hide it.

### 2.5 Follow-ups to expect

- "Why didn't you address that gap inside $employer?" — have a real answer; "*I considered it; the closest internal team is [team], but they're working on $different_thing*"
- "Have you talked to your manager about leaving?" — typically yes-or-no with a brief explanation; honesty
- "What would have kept you?" — what specific change at the current employer would have made staying the right call?

## 3. "Why This Company?"

### 3.1 What this question is really asking

Have you done your homework. The interviewer has had this question generic-answered ("you have a great culture") at least once today. A specific answer is rare and stands out.

### 3.2 The competency signal

- **Initiative.** Did you actually research?
- **Customer / Product orientation.** Do you understand what the company actually does?
- **Fit.** Does what you say you want align with what the company actually offers?

### 3.3 The structure that works

Three layers:

1. **A specific technical or product reason** ("the $product team's work on $area")
2. **A specific cultural or organizational reason** (something that's actually true about the company)
3. **A self-aware fit reason** (the connection back to your own trajectory)

### 3.4 Worked example

> "Three things — I'll keep them short.
>
> First, the technical: I read the $name_engineer post on $blog about your async-processing migration last summer. The decision to keep the consumer-side idempotency at the application layer rather than pushing it into Kafka itself is the same trade-off my current team is wrestling with, and reading how you arrived at it was genuinely useful.
>
> Second, the structural: I've talked to two engineers from your team — $name_one in the recruiter-arranged chat and $name_two through a mutual contact — and both said the same thing about how disagreements on the team get resolved: you write it down, you share the doc, and you decide in a meeting where the doc is the artifact. That matches how I want to work.
>
> Third, the fit: I've been moving toward higher-scale, higher-correctness systems for the last few years, and your team is doing exactly that. It's the natural next step rather than a sideways move."

This works because every claim is anchored — a specific blog post, a specific conversation, a specific behavior pattern. None of it is "you have a great culture."

### 3.5 Common pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Generic praise ("great company, great culture") | Heard from every candidate; zero signal |
| Reading from the careers page | Interviewer wrote half of that page |
| Compensation as the reason | Honest but doesn't answer "why this company specifically" |
| "I want to work on $tech" without naming what makes *this* company's $tech distinctive | Same answer to every employer |
| Mentioning a competitor's poor reputation | Reads as bitter and lazy |

### 3.6 Follow-ups to expect

- "What concerns do you have about working here?" — have a real, mild concern ready (e.g., "*the on-call rotation seems heavy; I'd want to understand it*"); pure-positive answers read as evasive
- "Where else are you interviewing?" — name 1–2 real comparables; if all-in on this company, say so honestly
- "What's your read on $recent_event at the company?" — if there's a publicly known event (acquisition, layoff, exec change), have a measured opinion

## 4. "What Are You Looking For in Your Next Role?"

### 4.1 What this question is really asking

Whether your stated criteria match what this role actually offers. A mismatch is the strongest predictor of an early-tenure departure, and the interviewer is calibrating that.

### 4.2 The competency signal

- **Self-awareness.** Can you articulate criteria, or are you "just looking around"?
- **Judgement.** Are the criteria reasonable for your level?
- **Fit.** Do they align with what's on offer?

### 4.3 The structure — 3 specific criteria, ranked

> "Three things, ranked.
>
> First, the technical scope: I want a role where the work is in the design-and-trade-off zone, not pure feature delivery. The clearest signal of that for me is whether the team has a written-design-doc culture and whether ICs at my level get to drive those docs.
>
> Second, the on-call shape: I'm willing to be on-call — I think it's part of the job — and I want a rotation that's manageable, with a real escalation tree, where pages typically come from real degradation rather than monitoring noise.
>
> Third, growth: I want to be on a team where the path from senior to staff is concrete. I'm not asking for a promise; I'm asking whether there are people on the team who've done that path recently."

Three criteria, named in order of priority, each with a concrete behavioral test.

### 4.4 What scores high vs low

| High-scoring | Low-scoring |
|---|---|
| 3 specific criteria with priorities | "Anything interesting" |
| Each criterion has a concrete test | Generic adjectives |
| Acknowledges trade-offs | Wants everything at once |
| Aligned with the role on offer | Mismatched (asks for things this role doesn't have) |
| Includes growth, not just pay | Only material things |

### 4.5 Pitfalls

- **Asking for things this role doesn't offer.** If you say "I want a tech-lead role" in an interview for an IC4 role with no TL component, you've signalled mismatch.
- **All-material criteria.** "Comp, work-life balance, remote." Honest but doesn't reveal anything about the work itself.
- **Hedging.** "I'm flexible on most things." Reads as no criteria, no priorities, no judgement.

### 4.6 Follow-ups to expect

- "Of those three, which matters most?" — be ready with a single answer
- "How would you know if you'd found the right role?" — concrete test ("after 3 months I'd want to have driven one design doc end-to-end")
- "What would make you turn this role down?" — willingness to name the deal-breakers; healthy
