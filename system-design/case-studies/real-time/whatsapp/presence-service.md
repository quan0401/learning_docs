## Summary

Presence — the green dot, "last seen at 14:32", and the typing bubble — looks like the simplest part of a chat product and is, at scale, one of the most expensive. It is **soft state with hard constraints**: low durability ("a missed heartbeat is fine"), low latency ("the dot must turn off within seconds"), enormous fanout ("each contact change can fan to N viewers"), and adversarial privacy semantics ("just came online" is a stalking signal). WhatsApp solves it by treating presence as a **completely separate path from messaging**, using **TTL'd Redis state** updated by **30-second heartbeats**, **subscriptions scoped to actively-viewing clients**, and **mobile-OS-aware backoff** so phones don't burn 4% battery per hour holding a socket open. This deep-dive expands the parent case study's brief presence section into the full design surface — what presence really represents, why polling and naive push both fail, the heartbeat/TTL contract, privacy tiering, and how iOS APNs and Android FCM force presence to "lie" gracefully when an app is backgrounded.

## Overview

The parent case study (`../design-whatsapp.md`) summarizes presence in roughly twelve lines: heartbeat every 30–60 s, TTL'd Redis entry, fanout-on-read via per-contact subscription, gossip across regions, deliberately separate from the message path. That summary is correct but elides every interesting tradeoff. Presence is interesting because:

1. **Volume is enormous and durability is zero.** Half a billion connected phones × heartbeat every 30 s = ~16 M ops/s of pure soft state.
2. **The natural design (broadcast every change to all friends) is unaffordable.** A user with 200 contacts coming online would generate 200 push events; multiply by churn rate and you're moving more presence packets than messages.
3. **The mobile OS will not let you keep a socket open the way you want.** APNs and FCM force *push-on-wake* semantics that don't fit "I am here right now".
4. **Privacy is not a feature on top — it changes the data model.** "Last seen: nobody" means the server must not even *return* the timestamp, not just hide it client-side.
5. **Multi-device makes "online" ambiguous.** The phone is asleep; the WhatsApp Web tab is open. Are you online?

The rest of this document walks each of those problems and the design choices WhatsApp (and similar systems — Slack, Discord, Signal) make to handle them.

## What Presence Is

Presence is the combination of several conceptually-distinct signals that users perceive as one thing.

| Signal | Semantics | Latency budget | Persisted? |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Online dot** | Connected socket exists right now | seconds | No (soft state) |
| **Last seen at** | Timestamp of last detected online transition | minutes | Yes (short retention) |
| **Typing indicator** | Active keystroke composition in *this* conversation | ~1–3 s | No (drop on reconnect) |
| **Recording voice** | Mic active in this conversation | seconds | No |
| **Away / idle** | Online but not foregrounded | seconds–minutes | No |

Each one is generated by a different client event:

- **Online dot:** the WebSocket handshake completes, plus a periodic heartbeat to refute "client crashed silently".
- **Last seen at:** computed from the most recent heartbeat the server saw before TTL expiry.
- **Typing:** a debounced burst when input field has changed in the last N seconds.
- **Recording:** mic permission granted *and* recording UI active.
- **Away:** OS-level "app backgrounded" notification, plus heuristics on input focus.

> **What you must NOT broadcast:** every keystroke. A naive implementation sends `KEY_PRESS` per character; that is a cellular-radio wakeup per character × N viewers per chat. WhatsApp instead emits a single `TYPING_START` debounced over ~1 s, and a `TYPING_STOP` either on inactivity timer or on send. The radio cost of sending one packet vs. ten is roughly the same on LTE/5G — what kills the battery is **how often you wake the radio out of low-power state**, not byte volume.

Presence is therefore a **rate-limited, debounced, semantically-aggregated** signal — never a raw event stream from the client.

## Why It's Hard at Scale

Three hard problems compound:

### 1. N × M fanout

Every user has up to M contacts. When user X transitions `offline → online`, the system must inform all of X's contacts who care. With 500 M concurrent users averaging 200 contacts each, the *theoretical* fanout is 100 B presence events per state change wave. In practice you reduce this by orders of magnitude using subscriptions (next section), but the naive worst case is what kills you if you forget the bound.

### 2. Polling vs subscription — both have failure modes

| Approach | Failure mode |
|---|---|
| **Client polls "is bob online?" every 10 s** | 50 M users × 200 contacts × 0.1 Hz = 10⁹ ops/s of pure read load — and average latency is half the polling interval. |
| **Server pushes every transition to every contact** | Burst on morning wake-up: 100 M users come online in a 30-min window, each generates ~200 fanout events → ~2 M ops/s sustained, peaks 10× higher. |
| **Pure long-poll** | One held connection per (viewer × contact) pair — connection count explodes. |

WhatsApp's actual approach, like most chat systems, is **subscription-on-view**: only push presence to the (small) set of users currently looking at this contact. That collapses the fanout from `N × contacts` to `N × actively-viewed-contacts`, which empirically is closer to 1–3 per user.

### 3. The "always online" assumption breaks the battery

A long-lived TLS WebSocket on a mobile radio costs roughly 1–4% battery per hour if held continuously. Multiply by 16 hours of foreground use and you've consumed half the battery on a "free" indicator. The protocol must accept that the client cannot maintain its socket continuously and must instead **come and go**, with the server inferring presence from the most recent heartbeat plus a generous TTL.

See [`../../../communication/push-vs-pull-architecture.md`](../../../communication/push-vs-pull-architecture.md) for the underlying push-vs-pull tradeoffs.

## Subscription Model — Don't Push to Everyone

The single most important optimization: **a presence update is delivered only to users who are currently viewing the relevant contact's chat or contact list**.

```
Alice opens Bob's chat
  ↓
Client sends:  SUBSCRIBE presence:bob
  ↓
Edge gateway adds Alice to bob's presence subscriber set
  ↓
On Bob's heartbeat / state change, gateway pushes update to all subscribers in set

Alice closes Bob's chat (or backgrounds the app)
  ↓
Client sends:  UNSUBSCRIBE presence:bob   (or TTL on subscription expires)
```

In pseudocode, the gateway-side pubsub:

```ts
// Gateway-side subscription registry — sharded by subject (the user being watched)
type SubscriberSet = Map<UserId, Set<SocketId>>;
const subscribers: SubscriberSet = new Map();

function onSubscribe(viewer: SocketId, subject: UserId) {
  if (!subscribers.has(subject)) subscribers.set(subject, new Set());
  subscribers.get(subject)!.add(viewer);
  // Send current state immediately so the dot doesn't lag
  const state = presenceStore.get(subject); // Redis lookup
  send(viewer, { type: 'PRESENCE', subject, state });
}

function onUnsubscribe(viewer: SocketId, subject: UserId) {
  subscribers.get(subject)?.delete(viewer);
}

function onPresenceChange(subject: UserId, state: PresenceState) {
  const watchers = subscribers.get(subject);
  if (!watchers || watchers.size === 0) return; // <-- the magic: nobody cares
  for (const sock of watchers) send(sock, { type: 'PRESENCE', subject, state });
}
```

The crucial line is `if (watchers.size === 0) return`. If nobody is currently looking at Bob's chat, Bob's coming-online event generates **zero fanout**. This is what makes presence affordable — most users' presence transitions in the middle of the day are observed by nobody.

A second-order optimization: subscriptions are **automatically expired** if the viewer's socket disconnects, so a crashed client doesn't leak subscriptions forever.

## TTL Heartbeat — Eventually-Consistent Online State

The contract:

- Client sends `HEARTBEAT` every **30 s** while it has a live socket and the app is foregrounded.
- Server stores `presence:{user_id} = {status, last_seen}` with a **TTL of 60–90 s** (i.e., 2–3× the heartbeat interval).
- If the TTL expires, the user is considered offline; the timestamp persists for "last seen at" via a secondary store.

This gives you:

- **No explicit "I'm leaving" message required.** Networks drop, batteries die, apps crash. The TTL handles all of them uniformly.
- **Bounded staleness.** "Online" can lag reality by at most TTL seconds. With TTL=60 s, a contact who has actually disconnected may still appear online for up to a minute. Acceptable.
- **Self-healing under partition.** If the presence cluster becomes unreachable, all keys eventually expire and clients re-establish on reconnect. No janitor process needed.

### Redis SETEX pattern

```redis
# Heartbeat from gateway, on every client heartbeat or any inbound message
SETEX presence:{user_id} 90 '{"status":"online","ts":1714200000,"device":"ios"}'

# Reading presence for a subscribe
GET presence:{user_id}

# Last seen — written once on transition (online→offline), longer retention
SET last_seen:{user_id} 1714200000 EX 2592000   # 30-day retention
```

`SETEX` is atomic and idempotent — repeated heartbeats just reset the TTL. The 90-second TTL with a 30-second heartbeat tolerates a single missed packet without false-flagging the user offline.

### Heartbeat client pseudocode

```ts
const HEARTBEAT_INTERVAL_FG = 30_000;       // ms, foreground
const HEARTBEAT_INTERVAL_BG = 5 * 60_000;   // ms, backgrounded — much sparser
const MAX_BACKOFF = 60 * 60_000;            // ms

let heartbeatTimer: number | null = null;
let backoff = HEARTBEAT_INTERVAL_FG;

function startHeartbeat() {
  stopHeartbeat();
  const interval = appState === 'foreground' ? HEARTBEAT_INTERVAL_FG : HEARTBEAT_INTERVAL_BG;
  heartbeatTimer = setInterval(() => {
    if (!socket.isConnected()) return; // socket layer will reconnect; do nothing
    socket.send({ type: 'HEARTBEAT', ts: Date.now() });
  }, interval);
}

function stopHeartbeat() {
  if (heartbeatTimer) clearInterval(heartbeatTimer);
  heartbeatTimer = null;
}

// React to OS lifecycle
onAppForeground(() => { backoff = HEARTBEAT_INTERVAL_FG; startHeartbeat(); });
onAppBackground(() => { backoff = HEARTBEAT_INTERVAL_BG; startHeartbeat(); });

// Reconnect with exponential backoff — never tight-loop a dead network
onSocketDisconnect(() => {
  setTimeout(() => socket.connect(), backoff);
  backoff = Math.min(backoff * 2, MAX_BACKOFF);
});
```

Two important properties:

1. **Heartbeats double as keepalives.** Any inbound message from this user (sending a chat, ACKing, reading) implicitly resets the TTL — the dedicated heartbeat is only needed during idle periods.
2. **Backoff escalates on network failure.** A phone in a tunnel that never reconnects must not retry every second; that drains the battery faster than the original socket would have.

## Privacy Controls

WhatsApp exposes three tiers for "last seen" and "online" visibility:

| Setting | Who sees presence |
|---|---|
| Everyone | Any contact can see online + last seen |
| My Contacts | Only people in your address book |
| My Contacts Except… | Allow-list with exclusions |
| Nobody | Last seen hidden; online dot also suppressed |

The non-obvious requirement is that **the server must enforce these, not the client.** A privacy filter that lives in the UI is trivially bypassable.

### Privacy-tier filter

```ts
type PrivacyTier = 'everyone' | 'contacts' | 'contacts_except' | 'nobody';
type PrivacySettings = {
  lastSeenTier: PrivacyTier;
  onlineTier: PrivacyTier;
  exceptions: Set<UserId>; // for contacts_except
};

async function canViewPresence(viewer: UserId, subject: UserId, kind: 'online' | 'last_seen'): Promise<boolean> {
  const settings = await getPrivacySettings(subject);
  const tier = kind === 'online' ? settings.onlineTier : settings.lastSeenTier;

  switch (tier) {
    case 'everyone': return true;
    case 'nobody':   return false;
    case 'contacts': return await isContact(subject, viewer);
    case 'contacts_except':
      return (await isContact(subject, viewer)) && !settings.exceptions.has(viewer);
  }
}

// Applied at subscription time AND on every push
async function pushPresence(subject: UserId, state: PresenceState) {
  const watchers = subscribers.get(subject) ?? new Set();
  for (const viewer of watchers) {
    if (await canViewPresence(viewer.userId, subject, 'online')) {
      send(viewer.socket, { type: 'PRESENCE', subject, state });
    }
  }
}
```

A subtler rule: **WhatsApp's symmetry principle.** If you hide your last-seen, you cannot see other people's last-seen either. This is a UX-driven anti-creep mechanism — it removes the incentive to set "Nobody" purely for stalking convenience.

Implementing the symmetry server-side is a single check during the read path: `if (subject.lastSeenTier === 'nobody' && viewer.lastSeenTier !== 'nobody') return null;` — the asymmetry is detected and the response sanitized.

## Storage Patterns

Three viable architectures, each with tradeoffs.

### A. Redis with TTL (WhatsApp's choice for hot state)

```
presence:{user_id}  →  {status, last_seen, device}   TTL 90s
last_seen:{user_id} →  timestamp                      TTL 30d
```

Redis Cluster sharded by `user_id`. Heartbeats are O(1) writes, reads are O(1) `GET`s. TTL gives free expiry. Memory is bounded by online-user count.

**Tradeoff:** No durability. A Redis node failure loses the live presence for ~1 % of users until they re-heartbeat. Fine — they'll reconnect within seconds anyway.

### B. Dedicated LiveData service (Slack, Discord)

A purpose-built in-memory service that maintains presence as part of the connection-management layer. The gateway *is* the source of truth — when a socket disconnects, the gateway knows immediately and emits `OFFLINE`. No TTL guessing.

**Tradeoff:** Tight coupling between connection management and presence. A gateway failover causes presence flicker. Excellent latency, harder to scale across regions.

### C. Client-managed gossip (peer-to-peer, less common)

Each client publishes its presence directly to the contacts it cares about, via a pub/sub overlay. No central registry.

**Tradeoff:** Discovery problem (how do contacts know which node to listen to?), poor offline handling, generally only seen in federated chat (XMPP, Matrix in some configurations).

WhatsApp uses (A) with regional shards and **gossip across regions** for a single user's presence — eventual consistency is acceptable because "Bob is online" being a few seconds stale across continents is invisible to humans.

## Battery Impact

A foregrounded client holding a WebSocket open with 30-s heartbeats costs roughly:

| Component | Battery cost (approximate) |
|---|---|
| Idle TLS socket on LTE | 1–2% / hour |
| Heartbeat radio wakeup (30 s) | ~0.5% / hour additional |
| Foreground UI render | 5–15% / hour |

Held continuously for 16 h, presence alone could consume ~24–40% of a daily battery. That is unacceptable, and WhatsApp doesn't do it.

**The actual strategy:**

1. **Foreground:** keep the socket open, 30-s heartbeats, real-time presence.
2. **Background, recently-active (last 5 min):** keep socket alive briefly, then let it close; rely on push notification to wake.
3. **Background, idle:** **socket closed**, presence allowed to expire via TTL, OS push channel takes over.
4. **Foreground burst on app open:** immediately re-establish socket, send aggressive heartbeat once, then settle to 30-s cadence.

So for an "average" user, the socket is open maybe 10–20% of the day. The server-side TTL machinery is what makes this work — without TTLs, you'd need explicit `GOODBYE` messages on every backgrounding, and you'd lose state on every dropped network.

## iOS / Android Background Limits

The OS doesn't let you do what you want.

### iOS

- **No long-running background tasks for chat apps.** Apple killed VoIP-style persistent sockets for non-VoIP apps years ago. PushKit (the VoIP push channel) is restricted to actual calls.
- **APNs is the only reliable wake mechanism in the background.** A push notification can wake the app for a few seconds (`content-available: 1` silent push), but the budget is small and Apple throttles abuse.
- **Background App Refresh is opportunistic.** The OS decides when, based on battery, network, usage patterns. You cannot rely on it.

### Android

- **Doze mode** batches network access into "maintenance windows" when the device is idle. Heartbeats stop.
- **App Standby Buckets** demote rarely-used apps; their network access shrinks.
- **FCM high-priority messages** can wake the app, but Google rate-limits abuse and may demote the app to normal priority.
- **Foreground services** are allowed but require a persistent notification (the "WhatsApp is running" sticky), and Google has been progressively restricting them.

### How presence "lies" gracefully

When the OS suspends the app, presence cannot reflect ground truth. Two strategies:

1. **The "last seen at" tells the truth.** If WhatsApp shows "last seen 2 minutes ago", that's the last heartbeat — accurate even if the user is technically still using their phone, just not WhatsApp.
2. **The dot is suppressed quickly.** The TTL of 90 s means the dot disappears within ~1.5 minutes of backgrounding, which roughly matches user expectation ("I closed the app, I'm not online anymore").
3. **Push-as-presence-trigger.** When a contact messages you while you're backgrounded, APNs/FCM wakes the app, which immediately re-establishes the socket and re-emits presence. From the contact's perspective, the dot turns on at exactly the moment you "saw" their message — even though technically you were offline 100 ms earlier.

This last point is why aggressive "presence is real-time" expectations are wrong. Presence is an **estimate** the system maintains as best it can, and the OS-level constraints make perfect accuracy impossible — so the design embraces approximation.

## Privacy at Scale — Presence as a Stalking Signal

"User X just came online" is, in aggregate, a **behavioral fingerprint**. Sleep patterns, work hours, anomalies (came online at 3 AM — fight with partner?), correlation across multiple contacts (X and Y come online within 30 s of each other, repeatedly) — all of this is extractable by a determined observer with a scraping client.

### Threat model

| Attacker | Capability | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Curious ex | Watches "online" dot manually | TTL coarsens timing; rate limits prevent excessive subscription churn |
| Stalker with scripted client | Polls/subscribes to victim's presence on schedule | Server-side rate-limit on subscription transitions per (viewer, subject) pair |
| State actor / bulk surveillance | Subscribes to many users via colluding accounts | Cap subscriptions per account; require contact-graph membership |
| Insider (employee with DB access) | Reads presence directly | Encrypted at rest, audited access, minimum-retention policy on `last_seen` |

### Concrete mitigations

- **Rate-limit subscription transitions.** A viewer cannot subscribe/unsubscribe to the same subject more than ~once per minute. This stops a script from sampling presence at 1 Hz to detect short online blips.
- **Coarsen "last seen" granularity.** WhatsApp rounds `last_seen` to the nearest minute, not second. A 1-second precision allows correlation; 1-minute does not.
- **Default to "Everyone"** — but make the privacy controls discoverable. Most users never change defaults; the defaults must be defensible.
- **Suppress presence for blocked contacts.** Blocking should make the blocker invisible to the blocked, in both directions, immediately.
- **Don't return last-seen in API responses for users who set "Nobody".** Not even null timestamps with a flag — *omit the field entirely* so the client can't infer "this user has it disabled". (In practice, the absence is itself a signal, but minimizing the leak surface is still correct.)

## Multi-Device Presence

WhatsApp now supports up to 4 linked devices (phone + 3 companions: web, desktop, tablet) plus a primary phone. "Online" becomes ambiguous.

| Scenario | What "online" means |
|---|---|
| Phone foreground, web closed | Online |
| Phone background, web foreground | Online (web counts) |
| Phone offline (airplane mode), web foreground | Online (web counts) |
| All devices closed | Offline |

The data model:

```redis
# Per-device presence
presence:{user_id}:{device_id}  →  {status, last_seen}   TTL 90s

# Aggregate (computed on read or maintained as a fanout)
presence:{user_id}              →  {status, devices: [...], last_seen: max(...)}
```

Two implementation choices:

1. **Compute on read.** Every read does `KEYS presence:{user_id}:*` (or a sharded variant) and aggregates. Simple, but `KEYS` is not viable at scale; you'd use a `SET` of device IDs as an index.
2. **Maintain aggregate on write.** Every device heartbeat updates both the per-device entry and a shared aggregate (`presence:{user_id}`). The aggregate's `status` is `online` if any device is online; the `last_seen` is the max of all devices' last-seen.

WhatsApp's multi-device architecture (post-2021) leans on (2) because a primary device has historically been the source of truth for the encryption ratchet, and the aggregate naturally falls out of the existing fanout.

A subtle UX decision: **"last seen" is the max across devices.** If your phone died at 10:00 but your web was open until 11:30, contacts see "last seen 11:30". This is correct from the user's perspective ("when did I last interact?") but surprising if you assumed `last_seen = phone_last_seen`.

## Anti-Patterns

Things that look reasonable and aren't:

- **Persisting heartbeats to the message store.** "Just write every heartbeat to Cassandra" — you'll have 16 M writes per second of zero-value soft state crowding out actual message writes. Heartbeats belong in Redis (or equivalent) with TTL, full stop.
- **Broadcasting every keystroke as a typing event.** Battery murder. Debounce to one `TYPING_START` per ~1 s window, plus a `TYPING_STOP` on inactivity or send.
- **Push every presence transition to all contacts.** The morning-wake-up burst alone will melt your gateways. Subscribe-on-view is non-negotiable.
- **Polling presence from the client every N seconds.** This is what subscriptions exist to prevent. If you find yourself implementing polling because subscriptions "felt complicated", you've inverted the cost curve.
- **Keeping last-seen at second precision.** It's a privacy leak with no UX benefit.
- **Trusting the client to enforce privacy.** "We don't display the dot if user has it disabled" — but the dot is in the API response. Fix the API, not the renderer.
- **Symmetric presence visibility but asymmetric privacy enforcement.** If A blocks B, B must not see A's presence; A also must not see B's (otherwise B can confirm the block by inversion). Bidirectional enforcement.
- **Single-device assumption baked into the data model.** A `presence:{user_id}` scalar that gets overwritten by whichever device heartbeated last produces flickering presence as devices race. Per-device + aggregate is the correct shape.
- **Strong consistency for cross-region presence.** Quorum reads on every presence query is wildly over-engineered. Gossip with eventual consistency is correct.
- **No rate limit on subscription churn.** A scripted client subscribing/unsubscribing every second to detect short online blips will succeed. Rate limit (viewer, subject) transitions to ~once per minute.
